# My first proto-trekking adventure — Croatia ‘18
[Jannes](https://medium.com/@jannesnotes?source=post_page---byline--fb526205770b---------------------------------------)
Sep 26, 2023

In 2018, now a very long time ago, me and my then girlfriend wanted to go on a vacation. But as first-years university students we had minimal funds to play with so decided that backpacking was the way to go. I used a backpack I already had from Chiro-camps and filled it with as much food from home as possible, so I didn’t need to spend too much on sustenance there. Another big expense to tackle was the accommodation, but this cost is easily reduced by buying a tent and going to cheap campgrounds. Little did I know this would become my preferred way to travel for the coming five years (_at least_). That first trip thought me a lot of practical things, from how to pack my bag, to what to pack next time and what I needed to leave at home. Of course this was still a camping vacation and not a full-autonomy trekking mission, but the things I learned on this trip laid the foundations for how I want to spend my time in the outdoors going forward.

The goal for Croatia’18 trip was very simple: Have fun for a cheap price. In practice this meant looking for the cheapest flights (because other modes of transport would be too time consuming) to Zadar and spending the night there to visit the city. the historical centre is interesting and the ice cream cheap (refreshment was needed). But for me, the real fun begins outside of the city, so I took a relatively short bus ride from Zadar to Plidvice. Here I pitched the tent in a campground with minimal facilities and walked to the very end of the campground to get the most real outdoors experience I possibly could get. But I knew little to nothing about pitching a tent so the location probably wasn’t very strategic, because the next morning I already had a cold. I don’t know if it was because of the air-conditioning from the plane and bus ride, or because my sleeping bag wasn’t warm enough, or of any other possible reasons, but it was pretty annoying. Of course I would not let it ruin the trip and just dressed warmer for the next night.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:875/1*tB_vBtKWaTQKBpDy698OTQ.jpeg)

After a slow morning reading, it was time to visit the place I came to see. Plidvice national park is dotted with beautiful lakes and waterfalls. The then green-blue, then transparent lakes and rivers were a sight to behold, but it also made the national park a popular tourist destination. Since a very young age, when I went on vacations with my parents, we always tried to avoid the touristy spots, so this trip was not going to be any different. I walked on the least travelled paths and climbed the least attractive hills to get to see the most stunning views.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:875/1*UpNuv6e30UCTIUDCprhooA.jpeg)

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:875/1*H5N8ie-Rl6XarZdqfhVpbQ.jpeg)

Somewhere on a summit I enjoyed my sandwich with ham and cheese, what would become a staple travel meal for me. On the way back I had to take a bus, but it was still a few hours before the next one would arrive, so hitchhiking looked like a good alternative. I learned that hitchhiking works, but it depends on a lot of factors — I’ll probably write a post completely dedicated to hitchhiking, because I always see the same tips appearing everywhere, but not the ones that I found most useful. In this case it looked like people from Croatia, at least from that area, weren’t too keen on picking up two strangers, because every time I stuck out my thumb, I got a visible no waving finger back. As if saying it wasn’t allowed there. But of course I am more persistent than this, so continued, and eventually I got picked up by two North-American travellers in their rental car. Something I learned then, and that will always keep surprising me is how extraordinarily kind people are, without any reason for them to be. We talked about their travels and about my plans and eventually ended up at the campground where the tent was still neatly standing.

The next day it was time to go to the next national park; Paklenica. This is a more mountainous area than Plidvice. After spending the evening at the lake, eating a freshly cooked meal and taking a swim, it was again time to pitch the tent. The next morning it was finally time to go hike in the mountains. Although it wasn’t a demanding hike, I wasn’t very experienced at the time, only relying on the day hikes I was used to with my family. But this place made me love the mountains, this is all I want to do. It was also an attractive idea to me that I was doing not many people want to do, and it makes sense: Who in their sane mind would like to fatigue themselves, sweating up the mountain path with a backpack that didn’t feel that heavy a few hours ago but suddenly weighs a ton? Who would ever choose an activity that makes your muscles hurt the next day instead of a relaxing week at a beach or an all in hotel at the swimming pool? I knew I would.

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:875/1*gJTjkc7ttz_W1LWLoCIMJA.jpeg)

That is something I learned here. Going through hardship makes the accomplishment so much more worth it. Later I also understood that one is capable of a lot of things with enough willpower. Here you see me standing without any idea that one should wear technical clothing to make the hike more comfortable, but I didn’t care, I was just enjoying the scenery. (Yes my feet were hurting after lol)

![](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:875/1*2KBCaRJioR8ed5ojNGU7VA.jpeg)

After another day in the tent it was time to get back to Zadar, before going back home and start uni again.

I still had a long way ahead to begin to find myself, and who I want to be, but here I started exposing that I love nature, I love the mountains, the rivers, the lakes, the rain and the sun, the hardships and the slow mornings, the hot afternoons and the freezing nights. I love it all.

### What I learned (practically)

Pack light! I’ll probably never bring the following items on a trekking adventure again:

- canned food;
- snorckle gear;
- too many clothes;
- my every day watch;
- a heavy multitool;
- back-up gear for your back-up gear;
- an old school military gamelle.

Every trip has its theme song for me, often accidentally. On this trip it was [Neroche — Day in, day out](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=b3GtXowzNTs).
