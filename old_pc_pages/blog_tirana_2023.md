## Autumn in the Western Balkan — Tirana ‘23

This marked my first-ever visit to Tirana, the capital of Albania, following a short flight from Brussels at 5 pm. Albania is the first country I’m exploring in the Western Balkans.

My journey began with a small hiccup: I realized that I couldn’t use my mobile data as seamlessly as in the rest of Europe due to the absence of roaming. Fortunately, I set a cap on how much I could spend outside my subscription. To resolve this issue, I promptly purchased a local SIM card. A valuable lesson learned: being in Europe doesn’t guarantee hassle-free data usage.

After sorting out the connectivity issue, I took a taxi to my cozy apartment in the heart of Tirana, conveniently near Skanderbeg Square. My apartment was in excellent condition and thanks to a helpful video from the owner, it was easy to find. You can find the booking.com link at the end of this post.

With the day growing late, I embarked on a quest to find a charming place for dinner. My taste buds were craving seafood, so after some Google Maps research, I stumbled upon a small restaurant that served delectable seafood dishes (link provided below). For my meal, I opted for seafood linguine after enjoying a Greek salad as a starter. The staff was warm and friendly, and the best part was the affordability — less than 10 euros for a generous plate of pasta!

By now, it was time to head back to my apartment. I was still nursing a lingering cold from my previous trip to East Germany, which, incidentally, involved sharing a train ride with some rather cough-prone fellow passengers.

The next morning, I rose at 8 am, eager to explore the city. My first stop was Skanderbeg Square, one of Tirana’s most iconic landmarks. The square is surrounded by skyscrapers and modern buildings, interspersed with remnants of Albania’s communist past in the form of older structures. Skanderbeg Square pays tribute to the legendary Albanian hero Skanderbeg, who led the resistance against the Ottoman Empire in the 15th century. His statue stands prominently, symbolizing courage, unity, and the spirit of independence. This message is further depicted in a large mural nearby. The square and its vicinity are adorned with numerous works of art.

Afterwards, I ventured south towards the Pyramid of Tirana, making a quick stop to admire a work of art named ‘The Cloud’. Evidently, this installation was quite impressive in its heyday, but the passage of time had rendered its white structure grey, with its once-translucent sheets darkened by water and dust. A little maintenance could restore it to its former glory.

Continuing south, I reached the Pyramid of Tirana, a large white concrete pyramid with stairs leading up from various directions. The climb was rewarded with a breathtaking panoramic view of the city center, encompassing modern skyscrapers and the majestic Skanderbeg Mountains in the background. This building also houses a museum dedicated to the Albanian politician Enver Hoxha, who ruled Albania from 1944 to 1985. Albania’s feelings towards Hoxha and its communist history remain mixed.

Having skipped breakfast, I took a well-deserved break for pancakes and coffee. I had indulged in more than enough food and a healthy dose of fruit. I was now ready to explore the Grand Park of Tirana.

For me, this park was the highlight of the day — an expansive park surrounding a large lake. The park offered an abundance of amenities, including cafes, restaurants, outdoor fitness equipment, and plenty of benches (complete with trash cans). This allowed you to relax and connect with nature at your leisure. Historical and artistic wonders dotted the landscape, creating an inspiring atmosphere.

The park’s natural beauty was a sight to behold. The park’s vegetation was left mostly untouched, allowing the trees to flourish as they pleased. Paths were thoughtfully laid out, distinguishing areas for pedestrians and cyclists. When you grew tired of the neatly paved paths, you could venture onto an earth road leading through the forest to the water’s edge.

I spent a good part of the afternoon in the park before deciding to further explore the city. This time, I took a different route and was pleasantly surprised by the abundance of street art adorning every corner.

For a late lunch, I indulged in Albanian street food — shawarma with fries and a Greek-style salad. As the day was drawing to a close, I paid another visit to the square before returning to my apartment to unwind. In the evening, I sought a place for refreshments and had to procure some bottled water (since tap water here is not always drinkable). I enjoyed a homemade iced tea and a plate of “tapas” before retiring for the night.

Today, my explorations led me to a ski lift in north-eastern Tirana. However, I had to first decipher the city’s bus system, as Google Maps proved unhelpful. Bus stops were not marked on the map, making navigation tricky. Eventually, I figured it out and knew I needed to take Line 11, conveniently located close to my apartment.

But before venturing forth, I started my day with coffee and breakfast at Papa’s Café, a charming little café next to my apartment. I savored a mochaccino and indulged in carrot cake for old times’ sake. As always, the incredibly low prices amazed me, not that I was complaining. I quickly returned to my room to gather my things, preparing for my mountain adventure.

En route to Dajti Express, the ski lift that awaited me, I met an elderly Albanian lady on the bus. She was eager to engage in a conversation, although I couldn’t understand her language. With some gesticulation, interpretative dancing, and help from Google Translate, we eventually managed to understand each other. She shared her experiences of visiting Sweden and her affection for Albania due to its pleasant climate.

A little later, another lady joined the conversation, and her slightly better English skills facilitated smoother communication. Interestingly, her daughter and son-in-law now reside in Belgium, near Maasmechelen, and she was taking a flight to Belgium the next day. They explained which stop I should use to disembark the bus and reach the cable car.

I wasn’t the sole tourist planning to take this route, as a considerable portion of the bus passengers alighted at my stop. After a short uphill walk, I reached the cable car station. The round trip ticket cost 1,400 Lek, or 14 euros. The view from inside the cable car was breathtaking — I could see the entire city on one side and the rugged yet lushly forested mountains on the other.

After savoring my midday meal, I embarked on a quest for another pathway down the mountain. As luck would have it, I stumbled upon an even better vantage point. From here, I could take in breathtaking panoramic views that stretched from the skyscrapers of central Tirana to the serene beauty of yesterday’s lake and park, and even glimpses of the sea to the west!

Back near the cable car, I discovered an unexpected gem: BunkArt 1, a museum nestled inside a Cold War-era bunker. This extraordinary museum sheds light on Albania’s recent history, chronicling events from the early Second World War to the fall of the USSR and the communist regime in Albania. As someone not well-versed in the nuances of Albania’s contemporary history, I found this museum to be an enlightening experience.

The bunker itself is an impressive sight, and the museum’s exhibits are thoughtfully curated. As I meandered through its many rooms, I was taken on a chronological journey that narrated the tale of the bunker, the nation, and its political landscape.

What truly struck me was the authenticity of the costumes and artifacts on display, as well as the effort to present an objective, albeit complex, portrayal of the country’s political history. I couldn’t help but appreciate (albeit with a touch of sadness) how art installations were used to evoke the somber atmosphere of those times. The immersive sound installations added the finishing touch to the overall experience.

Next on my agenda is a visit to BunkArt 2 in the city center, a prospect that I’m looking forward to.

By the time I wrapped up my exploration, the clock already read 4 PM. It was time to head back to the city center, where I had my sights set on one of Tirana’s most renowned shopping malls: Ring Center. I had anticipated a straightforward journey, but as it turned out, I hadn’t quite mastered the intricacies of the city’s bus system. The trip ended up taking an hour, when walking would have been faster — not to mention the ever-present traffic jams in the city center. After indulging in some shopping, I made my way back to my flat to prepare for a delightful dinner.

Tonight, I yearned for a traditional dining experience, and I stumbled upon a hidden gem located close to my flat, and just a stone’s throw away from Skanderbeg Square. As I walked through the door, I was warmly welcomed by the waiter, who would later share a bit of the restaurant’s history with me.

This charming eatery has stood the test of time, boasting a 23-year legacy. While it used to bustle with patrons, it now sees only a few occupied tables. A noteworthy detail: they accept cash payments only. The waiter, however, assured me that dining here wouldn’t break the bank, stating, “It’s not expensive here.” I ordered a local beer and savored their Greek-style steak, preceded by an appetizer of grilled vegetables. The vegetables were a delightful combination of flavors, enjoyed on a slice of bread drizzled with olive oil. While waiting for my main course, I couldn’t help but admire the skillful chefs working away in the open kitchen, ensuring every dish was prepared with utmost cleanliness.

The perfectly sautéed paprika and sweet onions, coupled with delectable meat, surpassed my expectations. Even the fries held their own against the best Belgian counterparts. To top it off, the meal, including drinks, cost me less than 9 euros! I wanted to sample a dessert to show my appreciation, but my stomach had already been treated to an ample feast.

I wholeheartedly recommend Taverna Paidhaqe DAJKUA. By the time I returned to my room, it was already 10 PM, signaling the end of another fulfilling day. Tomorrow, I’m excited to explore more museums and other sights that I haven’t had the chance to visit yet.

As I welcomed the morning sun, I set out for my breakfast destination, pondering a visit to the Fortress of Justinian. However, I was uncertain about finding a suitable spot for a morning meal at the fortress. Upon arrival, it became apparent that the location was more geared toward evening dining. While breakfast wasn’t on the menu, I did spot a nearby café offering coffee and delicious panini with eggs. Afterward, I paid a brief visit to the adjacent shopping mall and captured some intriguing art installations.

My next stop was the National Museum, conveniently situated on the main square. Here, I not only purchased a book for myself but also selected a surprise gift for my brother (he won’t see this post, so the surprise is safe!). The museum’s exhibits are thoughtfully arranged in chronological order, beginning with the Iron Age in the region that now encompasses modern-day Albania. The highlights for me were the sections dedicated to the Illyrians during the Greek and Roman eras, with informative descriptions and translations in English (unlike the later periods). The museum delved into the Middle Ages, exploring the various rulers of Albania with a cultural-historical perspective, offering insights into Byzantine, Norsemen, Hohenstaufen, and Angevin campaigns. It then transitioned into the Ottoman period, ultimately focusing on Albania’s quest for independence. However, that newfound freedom was short-lived, as Albania once again faced foreign occupation, this time by Fascist Italy and Nazi Germany. The museum served as a reminder of Albania’s complex history, a journey punctuated by external influences and struggles for autonomy.

The museum itself was a bit old-fashioned, and it was clear that budget constraints had limited its development.

After this enriching historical interlude, I decided it was high time for a refreshing ice cream treat. But there was still more history to uncover, and my curiosity led me to BunkArt 2. As I made my way to the bunker-turned-museum, I stopped to pick up lunch and savored it in a nearby park.

In my opinion, the first BunkArt museum held a stronger allure than its counterpart, BunkArt 2, situated in the city center and thus attracting more visitors. What made the first one truly impressive was its long, eerie, and empty corridors, creating a haunting ambiance. However, BunkArt 2 wasn’t without its merits — the rooms were tastefully curated, and the informative signage added depth to the exhibits.

With an absorbing day behind me, I decided it was time to unwind. I retreated to my apartment for a brief siesta and lost myself in the pages of a good book. As the evening approached, my thoughts turned to dinner.

As I delved into my evening meal, I decided to commence my culinary journey with a delightful fish soup, followed by the sumptuous Bream as my main course. The flavors danced on my palate, and I complemented this seafood symphony with an Albanian beer. The dining experience was lovely, though the waiter seemed apologetic, as he believed there were notable delays in serving my meal. To be honest, I didn’t find the wait too bothersome, but his concern was evident as he offered multiple apologies. To my surprise, as he presented my main course, he extended an additional pint “on the house” to make up for the perceived delay. It was a kind gesture, even though I didn’t feel the need for compensation. The Bream was truly delectable, and the entire meal came at a reasonable price of just 8 euros. To cap off the feast, the waiter returned with a dessert, once again complimentary. I couldn’t help but feel a touch guilty for the trouble he had gone to, but it was a gracious and unexpected gesture.

Late in the evening, I set out to see the Pyramid of Tirana illuminated by night, a captivating sight.

With the promise of another day ahead, I looked forward to making the most of my final day in Albania. My to-do list included one more museum, a quick stop at the football stadium, and a visit to a local bookstore.

My last day in Tirana began with a mission to tick off the remaining items on my list. I kicked things off with a visit to Tirana’s Arena, a modern edifice adorned with striking red panels. From there, I strolled past several university buildings before arriving at the Checkpoint monument, situated in a park that still features remnants of bunkers from days gone by (pictures 2–4 capture the scene).

As evening descended, I decided it was time for dinner, and coincidentally, the weather took a turn. Rain began to fall, marking the first downpour of my trip. I swiftly crossed Tanner’s Bridge and headed to a nearby restaurant (you can find the link below). This charming eatery had quite the reputation, having hosted not only regular patrons but even presidents from Albania and Kosovo. I treated myself to a delightful Albanian starter and a Kosovar main course, savoring the flavors.

However, when I stepped outside, I encountered a rather unexpected sight — the rain had poured down so vigorously that the street drains couldn’t handle the deluge. The result was flooded streets, with pedestrians wading through ankle-deep water. Clearly, no one had anticipated such a downpour.

Urgently, I made my way to my next destination, the House of Leaves. The heavy rain had made this excursion a bit challenging, but I was determined to visit this museum. Much like the BunkArts, this museum offered insights into Albania’s history, particularly within the context of espionage and prisoner interrogation during Enver Hoxha’s reign. The museum showcased a remarkable collection of artifacts spanning the period from just after World War II to the early 1990s. The informative display boards provided bite-sized information, while thought-provoking quotes throughout the museum underscored the significance of such historical exhibitions.

This marked my final stop in Tirana, and it was a fitting conclusion to my exploration of the city’s history.

In reflection, my time in Tirana was thoroughly enjoyable. The city partly met my expectations, but it also surprised me on multiple occasions. I had envisioned a budget-friendly Mediterranean city, one where the vestiges of communism and past hardships would remain visible. A city that was still developing and lacked some modern infrastructure, yet easily connected to the beauty of nature just beyond its borders.

Indeed, Tirana lived up to its reputation for affordability. I relished full meals for under 8 euros and found comfortable lodging for just 20 euros per night. Yet, I was taken aback by the modern architectural landscape that adorned the city’s skyline. The ongoing construction projects hinted at a more contemporary Tirana on the horizon. In a few years, I expect the city to undergo a noticeable transformation. I was equally impressed by the city’s commitment to modernization, with open plazas, ample benches, art installations throughout the city, bustling shopping malls and sleek skyscrapers. While glimpses of the communist past still surface, Albania seems intent on embracing its history while striving for a brighter future. The shadows cast by the panopticon of the 20th century are acknowledged but framed within the modern leisure plazas.

For those seeking warm weather, delicious and affordable cuisine, and a generous serving of history, I wholeheartedly recommend Tirana. This city, nestled in the Western Balkans, offers a distinct experience and is well worth exploring. Until next time, happy travels!
